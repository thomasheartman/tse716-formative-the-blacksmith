In the section where the knight is first revealed and then slowly stands up and puts his helmet on, I've used a chord progression that ends with the very "superhero" set of chords, known from the batman and marvel movies, possibly introduced by hans zimmer [get 8bit reference in here and double check the chords. If the chords aren't the exact same, then check to see why they feel so super hero].

The uplifting feeling then fades into the bittersweet chord progression as the blacksmith lifts his arms to the side inviting the knight to strike. The music is somber, yet kinda hopeful, intended to invite the listener to wonder whether this is the blacksmith accepting his end or something else.

The sfx are so important in the section between the sword being driven through the blacksmith's stomach and the sword shattering, that I didn't want to fight it too much, so I've left it blank for now [review when I've come to the end; I might also just leave some very quiet chords]

The sword shattering boom sounds to me as if it's a low B, so I've adjusted the key of the song to make that fit with the harmony of the chord. Of course, it's not a clear or particularly pitched sound, but it's just a little bit smoother that way.

Lyrically, I wanted the piece to feel ambiguous, but it's told from the blacksmith's point of view. I've understood this as if the knight is far from the first one who's come knocking on the blacksmith's proverbial door. Judging from the arrows displayed in the village, this is a semi-regular occurrence, and I think the blacksmith is kinda over it. The way I see it is that the real people who have an issue with the blacksmith aren't the ones who come face to face with him. Instead, they send mercenaries and adventurers, none of whom really have the full context of what is going on. In a way, they're pawns in a game they don't even know that they're playing.

Reference to Julius Cesar, Otto van Bismarck "the die is cast in iron and blood" subverting the expectation and meaning of it.

Also subverting the quote "you either die a hero or live long enough to see yourself become the villain" by making it more blacksmithy.
